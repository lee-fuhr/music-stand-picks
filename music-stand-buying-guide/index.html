How to choose music stands

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a music stand, first decide where and how you&#8217;ll use it: a sturdy, heavy stand for home or stage, or a lightweight folding model for travel. Then prioritize the features that matter most to you&#8212;adjustable height and angle, a wide enough tray for your sheet music, and a surface that won&#8217;t slip or sag. Finally, set a realistic budget: the best value often comes from mid-priced stands that balance stability and portability without the premium of professional concert models.

What types of music stands are there?

Most music stands fall into one of three categories: portable folding stands, home or studio wire stands, and heavy-duty concert stands. Portable folding stands are the lightest and most compact&#8212;great for students, buskers, or anyone who carries their stand daily. Their trade-off is stability: they can wobble on uneven floors or in a breeze. Wire or solid-desk home stands are a step up in sturdiness. They often have a metal or plastic desk that holds the music flat, and while they&#8217;re heavier, they rarely fold down as small. Concert stands are the gold standard: steel construction, wide adjustable desk, and a locking mechanism that won&#8217;t drift down during a performance. They&#8217;re heavy and expensive, but they&#8217;re the last stand you&#8217;ll buy.

How important is the stand&#8217;s weight and portability?

It depends entirely on your routine. If you carry your stand to rehearsals, gigs, or lessons several times a week, every pound matters. Ultralight folding stands can weigh less than a couple of pounds and collapse to fit in a backpack. The sacrifice is usually in stability and desk size. For home-only use, weight is almost irrelevant. A heavier, wider-legged stand will stay put while you turn pages or mark up your score. Some standing musicians also prefer a heavier base to prevent the stand from sliding on a hardwood floor. Think honestly about your typical carrying distance and frequency before prioritizing ultralight designs.

What should I look for in a stand&#8217;s tray and desk?

The tray (the surface that holds your music) matters more than most buyers realize. Cheap stands often have a thin, narrow tray that bends under the weight of a thick book or a stack of loose sheets. Look for a desk that is at least 18 inches wide and has a rolled or reinforced front edge to prevent sagging. Adjustable desk angle is standard on nearly all stands, but the range varies. Some tilt only slightly forward, while concert models can go nearly level for conducting or flat for writing. Also check whether the tray has grooves or clips to hold pages in place. A bare metal tray can be slippery; a textured or coated surface is much more reliable, especially outdoors.

How much height adjustment do I need?

Most stands adjust from roughly 25 inches to about 50 or 55 inches, which covers seated and standing players of average height. If you&#8217;re very tall (over 6 feet) or plan to play standing with tall instruments like a bassoon or cello, look for a stand with an extended top height&#8212;many concert models go up to 60 inches. Short players or children may need a stand that goes lower than typical minimums; a few budget-friendly stands now offer a lower starting height. Also pay attention to the locking mechanism: a friction collar can slip over time, while a positive-lock clutch or thumb-screw holds the height securely. Test the action at a store if possible, or read owner feedback about whether the stand &#8220;creeps down&#8221; during use.

What about special features: lights, tablet holders, or extra width?

Built-in LED lights are popular for dimly lit stages or practice rooms. If you play often in low light, a stand with an integrated light saves the hassle of clipping on an external lamp. However, some built-in lights are dim or flicker; check that the light has a warm enough tone and doesn&#8217;t cast shadows on the music. Tablet or tablet-specific accessories are now common. Some stands come with a tablet ledge or a magnetic holder, while others offer add-on brackets. If you use sheet music on a tablet, make sure the desk can tilt far enough to let you see the screen at a comfortable angle. Extra-wide desks (20 inches or more) are useful for two-page spreads or oversized scores, but they make the stand heavier and less portable.

How much should I spend on a music stand?

You can find basic folding stands for very little money, and premium concert stands for a few hundred dollars. The biggest jump in quality happens in the mid range&#8212;around the price of a couple of pizzas. In that band you get a stand with a solid tray, positive-height lock, and decent build that will last a student through college. Spending more usually buys better materials (steel vs. aluminum, thicker desk), smoother height adjustment, and longer warranty. For the occasional at-home player, a budget stand is fine. For a working musician logging hours of daily use, a mid- to high-priced stand is a one-time investment that pays for itself in reliability and comfort.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a regular folding table stand for heavy music books?

Not for long. Most basic folding stands have a thin metal tray that bends under the weight of thick anthologies or spiral-bound books. If you frequently use heavy scores, look for a stand with a reinforced or extra-wide desk, preferably with a rolled front edge.

Are plastic music stands any good?

Plastic stands can be very lightweight and rust-proof, making them great for outdoor use or for young children. The downside is that the plastic joints can crack in cold weather or after repeated folding. For occasional indoor use, a quality plastic stand works fine, but it won&#8217;t match the longevity of steel or aluminum.

How do I keep my sheet music from sliding off?

Choose a stand with a textured or non-slip tray surface, or use music stand clips (small spring-loaded clamps). If the tray is smooth metal, you can attach a rubber mat or a piece of shelf liner. Tilting the stand slightly backward also helps, especially in a breeze.

What is the best music stand for a child?

For children, look for a stand with a low minimum height (around 24 inches or less) and a lightweight but stable design. A wire or solid-desk stand with a small footprint works well. Avoid cheap folding stands that tip easily when a child reaches for the music.

Can I use a music stand for a tablet or laptop?

Yes, but check the desk size and tilt range. Many stands now include a tablet ledge or are deep enough to hold a tablet in a case. For a laptop, you will likely need an extra-wide or reinforced desk, and you might need a separate shelf or tray to hold the laptop securely.

Do I need a music stand if I play by ear?

Not necessarily. But even ear players occasionally use lead sheets, chord charts, or notes. A small, lightweight folding stand can be handy for those moments without taking up much space. If you never read music, you can skip the stand entirely.

In shortChoosing the right music stand comes down to matching the stand&#8217;s strengths to your playing environment. A lightweight folder for the student hauling gear across campus, a sturdy wire stand for the pianist at home, a heavy concert stand for the touring pro&#8212;there&#8217;s a correct option for each situation. Focus on stability, desk quality, and height range, and you&#8217;ll end up with a stand that supports your music, not one that fights it.
